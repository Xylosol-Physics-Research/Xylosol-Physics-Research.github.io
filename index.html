About

I am an independent researcher driven by a foundational question: Can the stability of quantum systems—from molecules to nuclei to quantum information—be understood through a universal principle of symmetry matching?

My work proposes the “Magic Square Constraint” hypothesis, which posits that stable quantum states are analogous to odd-order magic squares—complete and self-consistent—while transient states resemble even-order magic squares. To quantify this, I introduce the Symmetry-Matching Mismatch Degree (SMMD), a novel criterion that unifies phenomena across scales.

My background in agronomy (B.Sc.) informs my approach: I seek universal patterns in complex, adaptive systems, free from disciplinary silos.

Research Vision

The core of my theoretical framework is the idea that stability emerges when the symmetry of a system's quantum state perfectly matches the symmetry demanded by its environmental potential. This match can be quantified, and deviations from it drive dynamical evolution toward stability.

Current applications under development include:

  • Reinterpreting chemical bonding (H₂ formation) as a symmetry-matching process.
  • Explaining nuclear stability anomalies (e.g., deuterium vs. tritium) via a generalized SMMD.
  • Providing a physical interpretation of quantum error correction as an adaptive minimization of SMMD.

Publications & Preprints

The Magic Square Constraint: A Universal Stability Criterion Based on Symmetry-Matching Mismatch Degree (SMMD)
Chang, Lien-Hsing
Abstract: This work introduces a novel theoretical framework that unifies quantum stability across scales. By analogizing stable states to odd-order magic squares and transient states to even-order ones, we define the Symmetry-Matching Mismatch Degree (SMMD) as a quantitative stability criterion. The framework is demonstrated through three paradigmatic cases: the formation of the hydrogen molecule (quantum chemistry), the stability contrast between deuterium and tritium (nuclear physics), and the adaptive process of quantum error correction (quantum information). The theory suggests that stability is not merely an energetic minimum but a manifestation of deep symmetry alignment.
